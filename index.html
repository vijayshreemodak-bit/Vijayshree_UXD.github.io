<section id="overview">

  <div class="section-inner">

    <div class="section-label">01 — Overview</div>

    <h2 class="section-title">The current site informs — but never guides</h2>

    <div class="overview-grid">

      <div class="overview-text">

        <p>The Manipur Tourism website had a fundamental mismatch between what users needed and what it offered. Travellers — especially first-timers — came looking for help planning a trip: itineraries, transport, safety, permits. What they found was a passive information archive, structured around government categories rather than human journeys.</p>

        <p>My goal was to understand where and why the experience broke down, then redesign it around a single clear product strategy: help travellers plan and execute a memorable trip to Manipur in under 15 minutes.</p>

      </div>

<!-- RESEARCH -->

<section id="research">

  <div class="section-inner">

    <div class="section-label">02 — Research</div>

    <h2 class="section-title">Two lenses: what works elsewhere, what users actually need</h2>

    <div class="research-grid">

      <div class="research-card rc-orange">

        <div class="rc-label">Competitive Analysis</div>

        <div class="rc-title">Four benchmarks, one clear gap</div>

        <div class="rc-text">I analysed Kerala, Meghalaya, Rajasthan and Incredible India tourism websites — evaluated on navigation clarity, homepage engagement, trip planning support, trust signals, and mobile responsiveness.</div>

        <ul class="rc-list">

          <li>Kerala, Meghalaya and Rajasthan all feature a prominent "Plan Your Trip" CTA on clean, visual homepages</li>

          <li>Meghalaya's regional imagery creates authentic emotional connection — a direct parallel for Manipur</li>

          <li>Incredible India excels at inspiring but lacks region-specific depth — making strong state websites essential</li>

          <li>Manipur had no structured planning flow and buried trust-critical info like safety and permits</li>

        </ul>

      </div>

      <div class="research-card rc-gold">

        <div class="rc-label">User Interviews — 5 Travellers</div>

        <div class="rc-title">Not a discovery problem. A planning problem.</div>

        <div class="rc-text">Most travellers discovered Manipur through word of mouth. The website's job wasn't inspiration — it was conversion: turning interest into a confident, booked trip. Three themes dominated every interview.</div>

        <ul class="rc-list">

          <li>Planning felt overwhelming without a structured itinerary scaffold</li>

          <li>Missing transport details and upfront costs created a "go/no-go" hesitation</li>

          <li>Safety and accessibility info strongly affects decision-making — especially for first-timers</li>

          <li>Mobile is the primary browsing device — the current site was broken on mobile</li>

          <li>Static, text-heavy pages felt outdated and untrustworthy</li>

        </ul>

      </div>

    </div>

  </div>

</section>

<!-- LEARNINGS -->

<section>

  <div class="section-inner">

    <div class="section-label">08 — Learnings & Next Steps</div>

    <h2 class="section-title">What this project taught me</h2>

    <div class="learnings-list">

      <div class="learning-item">

        <div class="learning-idx">01</div>

        <div>

          <div class="learning-title">Simplicity is the design decision</div>

          <div class="learning-text">The biggest insight from 4 weeks of work: too many steps or complications drive users away. Every screen I cut, every field I merged, and every label I simplified made the flow measurably better in testing. Restraint is a skill.</div>

        </div>

      </div>

      <div class="learning-item">

        <div class="learning-idx">02</div>

        <div>

          <div class="learning-title">Trust is a UX problem, not just a content problem</div>

          <div class="learning-text">Users didn't distrust Manipur — they distrusted the website. Surfacing safety, permits and updated content early in the journey is an architectural decision, not just a copywriting one. Where you put information signals how much you respect the user's anxiety.</div>

        </div>

      </div>

      <div class="learning-item">

        <div class="learning-idx">03</div>

        <div>

          <div class="learning-title">Next steps: personalisation, transport depth, and regional authenticity</div>

          <div class="learning-text">The path is correct — but there's a long way to go. Next iterations would deepen transport details with estimated costs, add authentic visual storytelling (local vlogs and blogs), and test the experience with international visitors who face a higher planning barrier.</div>

        </div>
